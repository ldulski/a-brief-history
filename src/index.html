A Brief History of Floriography

Floriography is the language of flowers. It’s spoken by gifting specific flowers with associated meanings to communicate emotions. It’s utilized to send messages of love, friendship, admiration, warnings, loathing, or grief.

Origins of Floriography

Lady Mary Wortley introduced floriography to England during the Victorian era, between 1837 to 1901. Lady Wortley was an aristocrat and poet married to the British Ambassador of Turkey. She wrote letters describing the customs and culture of Turkey, specifically the secret ways of communicating through flowers.

Foxglove

Meanings: Riddles & Secrets

Its name comes from the belief that fae folk or fairies would hide within the blossoms. Foxglove is often associated with folklore in the British Isles. It’s bad luck to pick foxglove and is poisonous to eat.

Useful Flowers

Medicinal flowers

Chamomile

Chamomile is not only a beautiful flower but a medicinal herb. It’s best known for its calming properties for anxiety and settling upset stomachs. It can also be used to help heal minor wounds and prevent gum disease.

Global

Flowers from around the world

Red Spider Lily

Meanings: Passion & Sad Memories

Red Spider Lilies are radiant red flowers that bloom in the summer. Typically found around cemeteries, Red Spider Lilies are extremely poisonous. In Japan, they used to be planted on top of graves to stop wild animals or grave robbers from disturbing a loved one’s tomb.

Marigold

Meanings: Grief

Marigolds are used to celebrate “Dia de los Muertos” or the Day of the Dead in Mexico. When nightfalls the petals of a marigold curl inward and the head droops. In sunlight, dew will collect on the petals giving the flower the appearance of crying.

Plum Blossom

Meanings: Perserverance & Hope

The plum blossom comes from the Chinese plum tree native to southern China. It’s close to the apricot tree, and the fruit is sometimes called a “Japanese apricot.” These flowers bloom in early spring, just before the cherry blossoms.

Folklore

The stories and myths surrounding flowers

  • Anemone

    The anemone sprung from the Greek Goddess Aphrodite’s tears. Aphrodite is the goddess of love & beauty. She mourned the loss of one of her lovers Adonis who was killed by jealous gods who were envious of his relationship with the goddess. In other myths, fae folk were said to sleep in the flower’s petals at night.

  • Nettle

    A plant with several names, such as stinging nettle and Devil’s apron, nettle has acidic hairs all over the stem and leaves, causing pain if you touch them. According to Celtic folklore, if a person found a thick clump of nettles, fairies were living close by. Also, the sting of nettle could protect against fairy mischief and other forms of magic.

  • Narcissus

    The story of Narcissus is a Greek myth that follows a young man who angered the gods. The Gods caused Narcissus to fall in love with his reflection in a pool of water, and he couldn’t look away from his beautiful reflection. As a result, he died in that exact spot, never looking away from himself, and the flowers are known as narcissus bloomed where he died.
